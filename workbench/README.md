# Workbench (Step 1) · Daily Prompts

This is the "workbench" for the summer — no coding needed. Just copy-paste these
prompts into Claude each day. (Prompts adapted from the reviewed handbook.)

**About me (for the prompts):** a 14-year-old student from China, intermediate
English (TOEFL ~89), loves finance, stocks, and markets.

---

## Daily routine (~20–30 minutes)

1. Open Claude, paste **[Prompt 1 · Daily Pick]**, and send.
2. Claude gives today's article (title / source / link) + why it picked it + 5 key
   words + 2 guiding questions.
3. Click the link and read the original (real writing is the best English model).
4. Write **3–5 sentences to a short paragraph** in English: what you learned + what
   you think.
5. Say it out loud in English for **1–2 minutes**, then use **[Prompt 2 · Polish]** to
   let Claude edit it. Save the link + your reflection into `notes/YYYY-MM-DD.md`.

Once a week, update the simulated portfolio with **[Prompt 3 · Portfolio Review]**.
Any time you practice speaking, you can also use **[Prompt 4 · Speaking]**.

---

## [Prompt 1 · Daily Pick]

> Copy the whole block and send it to Claude. First time, tweak the details in
> brackets to fit.

```
You are my English + finance study assistant. I'm a 14-year-old student from China,
at an intermediate English level (TOEFL ~89), and I love finance, stocks, and markets.

Please make today's "market note" for me, in this exact format:

1) Pick ONE real English financial article for TODAY, suitable for an intermediate
   learner, from these sources: Reuters / AP / CNBC / MarketWatch / BBC Business /
   Investopedia / Yahoo Finance. Give me the title, source, and a working link.
2) In 2 sentences, explain why it's worth reading and what it's mainly about.
3) List 5 key words/phrases: the word — a simple English meaning — the example
   sentence from the article.
4) Give me 2 English guiding questions to help me form my own opinion as I read.

After I've read the original and sent you my English reflection, act ONLY as an
editor: point out grammar / word-choice problems and suggest more natural phrasing,
but do NOT rewrite it for me. End with one sentence of encouragement.

Only give me the link and your guidance — do not paste the full article (copyright).
```

## [Prompt 2 · Polish My Reflection]

> After writing your own reflection, send this together with your text.

```
This is my English reflection for today. Please act ONLY as an editor — do not
rewrite it for me:

1) Go sentence by sentence and point out grammar or word-choice mistakes, and give
   more natural wording;
2) Tell me which sentence is well written;
3) Give me 1 small suggestion to improve next time.

End with one sentence of encouragement. My reflection is:
(paste your English here)
```

## [Prompt 3 · Portfolio Review]  *(once a week)*

> Use this when updating the simulated portfolio, to think through why things moved
> and whether your judgment was right.

```
I have a VIRTUAL simulated stock portfolio (not real money). Here are my current
holdings and this week's moves: (list your stocks, why you bought them, and how they
did this week).

Please act like a patient investing mentor:
1) Ask me 3 questions to help me think through the logic behind each stock;
2) Point out risks I might be overlooking (in words I can understand);
3) Do NOT tell me what to buy or sell — let me decide for myself.

Then help me turn this review into a short paragraph for my "Portfolio Diary".
```

## [Prompt 4 · Speaking Practice]  *(1–2 minutes, daily)*

> After explaining today's note (or a math problem) out loud, send Claude your points.

```
I just explained today's market note (or a math problem) out loud in English. Here is
what I said / my main points: (paste your English, or a few key points).

Please act like a patient speaking teacher:
1) Point out the 2–3 grammar / word-choice issues that most affect my expression;
2) Give me more natural phrasing I can copy next time;
3) Keep it short — within what I can remember. End with one sentence of encouragement.
```

## Where the articles come from (free, good sources)

Reuters, AP, CNBC, MarketWatch, BBC Business, **Investopedia** (friendliest for
explaining concepts), Morningstar, Yahoo Finance News — plus primary sources (Fed /
central-bank statements, company earnings press releases). If you're willing to pay a
little, *The Economist Espresso* (a cheap daily briefing with excellent English) is
great for teens.

## Two red lines (must keep)

- **Copyright:** the site only links to the original article and shares the boy's own
  reflection — never reposts a full article.
- **Real money:** the portfolio uses virtual money only, never real trades. Any
  account or money matters are held and decided by the parent.

## Reminders

- **Don't let Claude write your reflection** — it only edits. Writing it yourself is
  the practice.
- Stuck? Ask Claude: "Explain this sentence in simpler English."
- Save one note every day. Consistency beats perfection — a small note every day for
  two years beats a fancy start you quit.
